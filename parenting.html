  • Returning to work with peace of mind Perhaps you are soon going to return to work after a well-earned period of maternity leave. This first period of separation should not be cause for concern. With a little organisation, it can be child’s play! Preparing your child for the change
    It’s important that you:
    *    Trust the person caring for your baby
    *    Accept that someone other than you can lovingly look after your child
    *    Openly ask any person looking after your child the necessary questions to dismiss your concerns

Me' Time

أكثر
  • Finding time for you. After weeks of focusing on your baby, a few hours devoted to some exercise or spending time with your partner or friends does not mean you are abandoning your child! If you are having trouble convincing yourself, here are a few good reasons to make the effort:

    Taking up exercise again
    Physical exercise is good for your body, as well as making you feel good by spending time dedicated to your wellbeing. The gym, stretch class, aqua-aerobics or yoga – the main thing is that you regularly set time aside. It will give you peace of mind. But do see your doctor if you are thinking about taking up exercise again after your pregnancy. And make sure you focus on re-educating your pelvic floor and abdominal muscles first.

    How to put this into practice
    Work on your abdominals without putting yourself in danger. Going to the gym and swimming are particularly well suited. Start gently but push yourself until you experience a burning sensation – the sign of an effective workout. Your time is precious: opt for an activity that you can practise near your home or work.

    Finding time with your friends and partner
    Here are just a few reasons why this is a good idea:
    *    Happy and loving parents are the best gift you can give your baby!
    *    Breaking up your routine is a breath of fresh air to make those inevitable dull tasks more bearable.
    *    Listening to the concerns of other parents will help put your own worries in perspective.
    *    Sharing your troubles with friends will help unload your worries without the fear of being judged.

Questioning Authority

أكثر
  • Babies over 15 months have acquired a sense of independence. It’s displayed in the moments they refuse to co-operate, which may happen more and more frequently. Faced with this new situation, every parent will adopt an attitude based on factors including: their own up-bringing, their personality, their partner’s personality and the attitude you have already adopted with any older children in the family. In short, the way in which you set your child’s limits is an important question to which each couple will provide their own unique answer.
    We have collected experiences from parents on how they applied their parental authority. Their complementary perspectives may help you to find the best approach for you and your child.
    Fouad and Mariam, parents of Tarek, 20 months
    Well explained authority works!
    ""Brett is a very active little boy. Right from when he first started walking, he wanted to touch everything in the house. Life was getting impossible because we constantly had to watch him and tell him not to touch things over and over again.""
    ""At first we tried to put everything out of his reach, but there were always things we hadn’t seen! One day, we decided to explain to him calmly but firmly that there were things within his reach that were not for him. We took the time to walk round the flat with him, showing them to him. Brett understood very well that it wasn’t a game. I think the fact that we were both there made an impression on him.""
    ""Since then, he has sometimes touched 'forbidden objects' but it’s very rare, and he seems to be even happier, that he can behave like an adult, than us!""
    Nadim and Hala, parents of Roula, 16 months
    Above all, don’t take different stances!
    ""For some time now, Jenny has been refusing everything. Whether it’s changing her nappy, getting dressed, taking her bath or eating anything other than pasta, her response to every suggestion is the same: 'No!' ""
    ""Faced with this obvious need for opposition, we took a stance of not clashing with her. We think that if our child systematically rejects everything we ask of her, it won’t do any good for us to insist on everything as well and just do as she does. We need to be able to show the example of flexibility and let her see that one can make compromises. The main thing was to set priorities to maintain a pleasant family life."" Houssam and Sara, parents of Naji, 18 months
    Exerting your authority at the right time can be reassuring!
    ""When he was coming up for 15 months, Ben suddenly became very difficult at mealtimes. He refused to try new foods. He wanted to eat with his fingers, even though he used his little spoon and fork correctly before. Rather than accepting or opposing everything, we chose to be firm on some points, and lenient on others. Ben must eat the foods he likes with his spoon and he must try at least a mouthful of foods he doesn’t know. In return, he can take certain 'permitted' foods with his fingers; little bits of ham or fruit for example, and he doesn’t have to finish his plate. What we actually did was just set new rules for the game. These clear and simple rules have certainly given our Ben a feeling of security.""

Eating together

أكثر
  • The many pleasures of mealtimes.
    Eating is an essential human function. Every country and every civilisation has created its own eating habits and cuisine. Teaching healthy eating habits to children is something parents may take for granted but it can sometimes be difficult when our children refuse to eat certain foods. The pleasure of mealtimes plays a fundamental role in passing on healthy eating habits to our children. Leading by example at meal times will also encourage your child to develop the healthy eating habits you want them to.
    Enjoyable mealtimes for you and your child
    By eating together and enjoying your family meal, you are:
    *    Encouraging your child to imitate you
    *    Showing your child that eating is something enjoyable and entertaining
    *    Treating yourself to better eating habits
    How do you do it?
    *    Find quick and easy recipes, and always have the basic ingredients on han
    *    Settle down comfortably at the table and take your time
    *    Lead by example – children love to see their parents doing the same thing they are
    Some practical tips:
    When you can, make meals with your child:
    Show your child what you’re doing and explain things as you go along. Get them to take part (even if they are very small, they can go to the shops with you to find out about fruits and vegetables). Saying the names of the foods you are including assists in their learning and they will see that those foods are the ones we eat most often in a healthy diet.
    A child’s sense of taste is not the same as an adult’s; it is often more sensitive. This is not a reason to limit children’s exposure to strong-flavoured foods, but instead, offer them in small amounts so that they get used to them.* Offer any new foods with foods you know your child likes – it will increase their success and won’t limit them to just the new food to eat.
    If your child likes a food:
    Repeat the experience several times, presenting it in the same way, so that your child takes pleasure in finding the food again and remembering it. Then, later, you can vary the same recipe and come up with different presentations.
    Make your child like vegetables:
    Some young children don’t like to eat fruit and vegetables, at least initially. You can help them through this phase by: Offering them fruit and vegetables regularly and in a variety of ways. Children eat what is familiar to them. Including new fruit and vegetables in already accepted recipes.
    Involving them in the decision making process – what fruit would you like for your snack? What vegetables would you like with your dinner tonight? Praise your child whenever they try them – even the smallest amount.

    * Babies should not be given solids until they are around six months old, and definitely not before four months.

Baby Safety

أكثر
  • Keeping safe is just a question of intelligent planning. The list of potential dangers to your baby may seem endless, but fortunately domestic accidents can nearly always be prevented!
  • Protect your baby
    • You want to child-proof your house – and especially your kitchen? Just follow these basic security rules. Your whole family will benefit from the changes you make. Your peace of mind will improve considerably too.
      Remember, you don’t need to be over-cautious. You just need to be aware of all the possible accidents that could happen – and stop them before they do!
  • Preventing Domestic Accidents
    • It’s important to make sure your baby is safe every day by looking at everything around you through the eyes of a child. Here are a few precautions to take for the good of your child’s wellbeing:

      When your baby is on the changing mat, never leave them unsupervised and always keep one hand on them – babies like to wiggle about when being changed and can easily fall.

      Never leave your child alone in the house or with another child. Never leave them alone for even a moment in the bath or a child’s swimming pool. You would be amazed how little water can pose a danger of drowning.

      Always lay your baby on their back to sleep.

      The temperature of your child’s room must never go above 18°C, even if this feels cold to you. Put your baby in a babygro to sleep. Quilts and other covers are not advised until your child is one year old.
      Keep the baby’s toys together at the foot of the bed.
      Always check the temperature of the food you give to your child.
      Never leave your child alone with an animal.
      As soon as baby starts to walk, remove everything from reach that is potentially dangerous for baby to swallow: Mum’s rings, peanuts and olives left from a snack, plant pots filled with soil. Babies like to put everything in their mouth – even if it’s not good for them!
      Never let your child run with something in their mouth.
      Remember to tidy away cleaning products, medicines and cosmetics in cupboards that your little explorer is unable to open.
      In the kitchen, make sure knives, appliances and plastic bags are out of reach.
      When crawling about, your baby will love to use both hands to grab onto anything it can use to pull himself up. Protect your baby from burns by fitting a protective grill on your oven door and always point saucepan handles on the hob towards the back wall.
      In old houses, make sure your child avoids eating flakes of paint, which can contain lead.
      Making your home safe for baby also entails putting a gate across the stairs, covering over electric sockets, blocking windows and doors, closing off balconies and swimming pools, protecting sharp corners on furniture, checking heating apparatus, etc.
      Neighbour’s homes can pose a danger too, especially after your child begins to walk. Make sure that your child is not able to get into your neighbour's house without adult supervision. If this is not possible, be sure to maintain the friendly relations with them that enable you to prevent your child encountering unexpected dangers on their property.
      As your child grows up, you need to be constantly vigilant – without becoming paranoid of course.

Make Travel Fun

أكثر
  • Travelling with baby can be a real challenge - or a real pleasure if you prepare everything well in advance! Make a simple checklist of the items you need to take with you, and check the weather conditions predicted for the big day.
    If you’re travelling abroad, be sure to have all the appropriate documents, both for yourself and your baby. Take care to pack important medication ifou’re not sure you can find it in the country you’re travelling to.
    If your baby or toddler is using formula, you should be aware that infant formulas overseas have different formulations and so the taste varies. It may be a good idea to bring your own formula.
    Going away with your baby is often the opportunity to spend time with all the family together in a relaxed atmosphere. But to make your trip a success, good preparation and organisation are essential.
  • Packing for baby
    • Don’t bring your baby’s entire wardrobe away with you! Even if your little one does look cute in a woollen jumper, thick sweaters are pointless if you are heading to sunny climes. Only take essential items: seasonal clothes, toiletries, nappies, baby wipes and those other useful “tools” for a mother who thinks of everything! Take pity on Dad who may be out of practice packing a suitcase!

      Baby equipment (collapsible cot, pram, etc.) can be bulky, so leave it all behind if you can hire accessories at your destination. Some hotel chains provide everything for baby guests – enquire before you leave.
  • Tips for a Stress Free journey
    • If you leave with nothing else but nappies and food in good quantities, then you have the essentials! A fed baby is a calm baby, so make sure you feed them before leaving. And travelling at night will avoid multiple toilet stops and other disruptions.
      If travelling by train, find out if the rail system offers a designated family area, and reserve seats for the comfort of the whole family. Little ones usually won’t be allocated their own seats but will sit on your lap. Rail companies usually offer reasonable prices for older children so make sure you ask.
      When flying, breastfeed (the best choice) or bottle feed your baby on take off and touch down to prevent their ears popping. When driving, whatever your means of transport, you should easily be able to stop to change your baby and heat their meal. Nevertheless, always keep food, a small cover and security blankets close to hand – and remember to give your child something to drink regularly and make frequent stops along the way.
  • Driving to your destination: Safety first
    • In the car, it’s vital for babies’ safety to seat them in equipment that is specially adapted to their height and weight and complies with the local safety laws. If your baby is not able to sit upright, you have the choice between baby carriages and seats facing away from the front. For older children, harness seats are recommended, but make sure you attach the seat correctly, respecting safety regulations, and also secure baby properly.
      Stowing luggage can often cause a headache. But even if the boot is full, it’s a good idea to keep the back shelf completely free. This will prevent items flying off if you need to brake suddenly and will keep items out-of-reach of baby. When the car is full, leave just after you have fed your baby or during the evening, so baby can sleep and you can avoid traffic jams. If you are travelling in summer, set the air conditioning to around 7°C below the temperature outside and attach sun visors to the windows. On the road, make stops as often as possible and give your baby lots of fluids. And NEVER leave baby alone in the car – even for a few minutes. Bon voyage – and just think, next year you’ll be the one giving out advice!

The winner is the player who pins the nose closest to the correct position.

أكثر on Nutrition

أكثر
  • Giving your little one the best nutrition can be challenging. You’ve come to the right place! Here you will fill find lots of useful information and learn more about nutrition for your toddler or preschooler.

  • Toddler’s Menu: Colorful, Tasty and Healthy!
    • What is healthy eating?
      Healthy eating for your child means giving him a variety of foods so that he gets the right nutrients (protein, carbohydrate, fat, vitamins, and minerals) for healthy growth.
      The best way to ensure your child gets what he needs is to provide a variety of nutritious foods that are low in fat and sugar. Meals and snacks should be offered at intervals not more than 3 hours apart during the active part of your child's day. Components of meals and snacks should be planned so that they’re appetizing and provide adequate servings from the milk, yogurt, and cheese group; meat, poultry, fish, dry beans and egg group; vegetable group; fruit group; and the bread, cereal, rice and pasta group.
      How much food is good for your child?
      Food selections should be based on balance, variety and moderation. For example:

      Breakfast Snack 1 Lunch Snack 2 Other
      1 cup of specialized milk for toddlers  ½ cup fresh fruit juice ½ cup cooked vegetables ½ cup fruit salad 1 cup of specialized milk for toddlers 
      ½ bowl of breakfast cereals   ¼ cup meat, chicken or fish    1 small labneh sandwich
      1 boiled egg   ½ cup potato, pasta, rice, bulgur, corn… (starches)   ½ cup tomato, lettuce

      The number of portions in the above menu is in line with the recommendations for children 1 to 3 years of age:
        Milk and dairy products: 3 portions (recommended 2-3 portions)
        Grains and cereals: 6 portions (recommended 4-6 portions)
        Meat and legumes: 2 portions (recommended 2 portions)
        Vegetables: 3 portions (recommended 2-3 portions)
        Fruits: 3 portions (recommended 2-3 portions)
      Help your child get enough dairy
      Aim for 3 servings of milk, yogurt or cheese a day as it is a good source of calcium for healthy and strong bones.
      Make half his grains whole
      Make sure that at least 1/2 the quantity of the total grain requirement comes from whole grains (Ex. Bulghur, whole wheat bread)
      Vary the Veggies you offer him
      Aim to provide him with this quality every week:
      -     Dark Green Vegetables (ex. parsley, broccoli, spinach..) = 1 cup weekly: a good source of vitamin A, C and         iron
      -     Orange Vegetables (ex. carrots) = 1/2cup weekly: a good source of beta carotene (vitamin A)
      -     Dry Beans & Peas = 1/2 cup weekly: a good source of fibre
      Aim for 3 teaspoons of vegetable oils a day
      Sweets only as treats
      Limit sweets to protect your child from tooth decay and excess calories that are poor in nutrients. Offer it to him only as treats to avoid him developing a sweet tooth.
  • Snacks: moderate, don’t eliminate!
    • Did you know that 25% of a child’s daily energy intake comes from snacking between meals? Unfortunately, much of this nibbling is on pre-packaged snack foods that are high in calories and low in nutrients.
      It’s not snacking that’s a bad thing, its unhealthy snacking. In fact your child needs to eat snacks.
      Snacks are essential
      The reason for this is that a toddler’s stomach is still pretty small, so he often won’t get all the daily nutrients and energy he needs from the three main meals alone. Snacks help fill this gap. Also, if you give your child healthy snacks now, you will help him learn to make healthy food choices in the future. This will help him avoid becoming overweight.
      Tips on how to provide your child with healthy snack options:
        Prepare homemade desserts that are nutritious and low in both fat and sugar
        Don’t overly restrict foods, as this can make your child feel punished or rejected. Any restrictive diet should be done under the guidance of a healthcare professional
        Offer some pre-packaged snacks like chocolates and biscuits, but in limited quantities
        Read the labels on the packages to be sure about the health value of the foods you are giving your child
        Wash fruits and vegetables and put them in the fridge so they are ready to eat
        Don’t use food as a reward or punishment
        Don’t offer snacks close to mealtimes
      Some healthy snacks you can offer your child:
        Milk with wholegrain cereal
        Fresh fruit salad
        Pudding and bread sticks
        A cup of milk with cocoa powder or a banana
        Fruit milkshakes (milk, strawberry or banana and a teaspoon of honey)
        Toast with labneh or cheese
      Offering your child a healthy snack is an important part of his overall food regimen, as it provides him with nutrients, sustains him until his next meal and gives him the energy to continue his activities
  • Nutrition Discoveries: Probiotics help build your child’s immunity
    • Yoghurt is well known as an important food for children because it contains probiotics –live cultures that help maintain the health of the digestive system. Recent scientific research supports this by confirming that probiotics not only support a healthy digestive system but also help protect the body from disease by fighting harmful bacteria.
      What does “probiotics” mean?
      The word probiotics comes from a Greek word meaning “friendly” bacteria. They are naturally found in the gut (tummy).
      The importance of probiotics in your child’s diet increases after he turns one year old. At this time, he has a strong desire to explore the world around him by touching and tasting everything he can reach. This exposes him to a lot of germs, bacteria and viruses. This is where probiotics come into play.
      The health benefits of probiotics in children
        Help reinforce gastrointestinal health
        Help protect the gastrointestinal system from harmful bacteria
        Help strengthen and boost the natural immune system in children
        Help protect children from many types of diarrhoea
        Can protect children from some kinds of allergies, according to research
        Help increase the absorption of minerals and vitamins in food
        Help reduce the symptoms of lactose intolerance
      Since keeping your child from falling ill is your priority, supplementing his diet with probiotics is a natural and easy way to help him stay healthy.
      What are good sources of probiotics?
      You can find probiotics in dairy foods that are rich in live cultures, such as yoghurt and cheese, as well as in foods and milks fortified with probiotics.
      Did you know?
      Probiotics help the digestive system recuperate after your child has been treated with antibiotics. Antibiotics kill the bacteria that cause illness, but they do not differentiate good bacteria from bad. That means they also attacks the beneficial bacteria that protects the gastrointestinal tract and maintains its health. So, if you provide your child with foods rich in probiotics for two weeks after any antibiotic treatment, you can help restore the level of beneficial bacteria and enhance the overall health of your child’s digestive system. Protect your child from illnesses during this time of discovery by boosting his immunity with a healthy and balanced diet that includes probiotics.
  • Learn about Prebiotics: for Your Child’s Digestive System
    • We know that fibre is good for your child’s digestive system, but did you know that prebiotics are too? So what are prebiotics and what role do they play in you child’s health? Prebiotics are carbohydrates that are not digested or absorbed in the small intestine but remain intact through the digestive tract to reach the large intestine where they essentially serve as food for the beneficial bacteria (microflora) that resides there. Alongside fibre, they play an important role in maintaining your child’s healthy digestive system.
      Did you know?
      Dietary fibre is found in vegetables, fruits, whole grains and legumes. Experts recommend giving your child a diet rich in dietary fibre. It regulates the digestive process, helps your child avoid constipation, keeps a balanced medium in his digestive tract and helps him avoid abdominal distress like abdominal pain, diarrhoea and infections. It is important that children more than one year old get plenty of fibre.
      Importance of prebiotics in child nutrition
      From the time your child begins to crawl, and as he learns to walk and run, curiosity drives much of his activity. He will touch, and even taste, whatever he can get his hands on. This exposes your child to all kinds of bacteria and microbes that can cause stomach upset, irregular bowel movements and diarrhoea that can be severe at times. Prebiotics play a role in treating constipation in children, and research shows that they also can help protect the gut from harmful micro organisms that can lead to intestinal infections.
      One good way to help your child stay healthy and keep his immune system strong is to choose his foods wisely and make sure that they include prebiotics.
      Good to know
      Prebiotics help improve mineral absorption and balance in the body
      Where can you find Prebiotics?
      Prebiotics are found naturally in fruits and vegetables such as tomatoes, bananas, berries, onions, garlic, artichoke, asparagus, chicory and spinach; it also is in grains such as wheat and barley; legumes, including lentils, kidney beans, chickpeas, navy beans and soy beans, and specialised milk fortified with prebiotics.
  • Pleasing your Picky eater!
    • Do you find yourself frustrated with your toddler’s food choices? Your son wants to eat only spreadable cheese sandwiches for all his meals; your 2 year old girl only eats pasta with tomato sauce and refuses any other meal you offer!
      Well guess what you are not alone! Picky eating is a phase most toddlers go through. It is completely normal for young kids to reject foods they have never tried before or to ask for the same food over and over again.
      As long as your preschooler is healthy, energetic and is growing normally for his age then he is getting his needs. So you need not get frustrated, picky eating is something most kids grow out of. It is just their way of showing their independence and it will soon fade out on its own.

      Here are some tips on how to deal with your picky eater and help him try new foods:
        Offer new foods many times. It may take up to a dozen tries for a child to accept a new food.
        Give them a small taste at first and be patient with them. Eventually their taste buds will get used to the new and they will learn to enjoy it.
        Be a good role model by trying new foods yourself. Show your child that you are eating the same and soon he will want to try for himself.
        Offer only one new food at a time. Serve something that you know your child likes along with the new food.
        Offer new foods first, at the beginning of a meal, when your child is still hungry.
        Involve your child in doing the groceries children enjoy help out in picking out produce.
        Involve your child in the preparation of the food; they are more likely to eat something they helped prepare.
        Offer a variety of foods for your child to choose from. Usually children go for the second one, so offer the one you want to give second. Example, do you want beans or broccoli?
        Cool down, do not get angry if your child refuses to eat a certain food or if he is restless at the table tell him to go to his room and do not offer food till the next meal.

  • What to do when your child won’t eat
    • When you see your child limiting his food intake, don’t worry; instead, be aware that this can happen and prepare for it. During this stage, toddlers are more interested in the new world surrounding them than they are in the food in front of them.

      If you are struggling to get your child to eat, try these tips:
        Don’t get angry. Take the food away and then offer it later.
        Never force your child to eat; he knows when he is full. Learn from him how much he eats and when he stops, because he knows best when he is hungry or full.
        Check with your paediatrician regarding your child’s development and growth, especially with regard to height.
        Notice whether your child is energetic and physically active. Sometimes fatigue and lack of appetite are early signs of illness.
        Always offer your child small servings of food, even if it seems like it won’t be enough; it’s almost always adequate for a child since their nutritional needs are much lower than ours.
        Prepare the kinds of food that your child likes and asks for. He can actively participate in choosing and preparing the meal with you.
        The most common mistake a parent makes is replacing the main meal with unhealthy snacks, if the child is refusing to eat. A better option is to refrain from giving your child anything to eat until he is very hungry. Then offer him a main dish of meat or chicken or a favourite side dish, like rice or yogurt.
        Many children eat better in a group of kids, so try to regularly invite your child’s friends over, or have lunch with relatives or neighbours.
        Buy plates, bowls and cups with your child’s favourite heroes or characters pictured on them. This is one way to encourage him to eat.
        A child’s tastes change every day. Never give up introducing new dishes to your child and exposing him to new tastes. What he disliked before, he may come to like later on.
        Cut food into the shapes and the sizes he prefers when eating it – such as squares or sticks or circles. Let your child eat with his hands or pick up the food with his own fork. Be flexible, and don’t impose your rules on your child. Let him set some of the rules.
      Use these tips as a guide for healthy eating and good food habits that ensure your child gets a well-rounded diet.

      Physical fitness is a state which permits the individual to carry out daily activities including physical activity without fatigue.Physical fitness include muscular strength & indurance, flexibility, cardiovascular & motor components.
      Low level of Physical activity & fitness are risk factors for chronic diseases & premature mortality, increase prevalence of obesity, metabolic & cardiovascular diseases.
      On the other hand, persistence of chronic undernutrition compromises physical activity & fitness.
      Regular physical activity does not alter linear growth & biological maturation but is important in the regulation of body weight & integrity of skeletal muscle & bone tissues.
      Energy & protein requirements to support normal growth are highest in early infancy & subsequently decline. After 2 years of age, only a small percentage of energy & protein intake supports growth, the major portion supports tissue maintenance or replacement.
      Chronic undernutrition in toddlers results in compromised growth, delayed development of proficiency in movement skills & reduced physical activity.

  • Childhood obesity – what can you do to prevent it?
    • Childhood obesity is a growing health concern around the world, just as it is here in this region. It can begin at any age and can lead to serious health complications, including sleep apnoea, increased risk of asthma, decreased bone density, difficulty in movement, increased risk of cardiovascular disease later in life, and difficulty building relationships with other kids.
      How can I tell if my child is overweight?
      Your child’s weight alone is not a reliable measure of obesity. You have to consider his height as well. A tall child can weigh more than a shorter child and still not be overweight. During your child’s regular doctor’s visits, his weight and height are measured. By assessing these numbers, you and your doctor can easily determine if your child has a healthy weight or is overweight.
      What causes childhood obesity?
      In 98-99% of childhood obesity cases, overeating is a contributing factor. Why? Simply put, a lot of children eat more food then their bodies need. Another factor is the low quality of food that children are eating at home and at school. High-caloric, high-fat and low-nutrition foods like chips, fast food and sweets can lead to unhealthy weight gain. Lack of physical exercise is another cause of obesity, since, without exercise, the body isn’t converting as much food to energy and instead is converting it to fat.
      Good to know
      Studies show that children who watch television or stay in front of the computer for more than two hours a day are more likely to become overweight. This sedentary behaviour also is normally accompanied by eating high-caloric, high-fat and low-nutrition foods.

      Follow these tips to prevent your child from becoming overweight:
        Get moving: Incorporate physical activity into your child’s everyday life. For example, get him more involved in helping out with chores around the house. Take him for a walk in the park and encourage him to take the stairs, whenever possible, instead of using the elevator.
        Pick a sport: Support you child in taking up one or more sports; go watch him compete, and be a role model by being active yourself. Exercise helps convert excess calories to energy and prevents them from accumulating as fat in the body. Sport also enhances your child’s social skills through competition and being part of a team.
        Encourage your child to eat right: Cut down on unhealthy snacks and foods that have lots of calories, fat and sugar, such as chips, ice cream, cakes and biscuits. Avoid fast food.
        A healthy and balanced diet incorporating all the food groups – milk and dairy, breads and cereals, fruits, vegetables, and meats and legumes – will provide your child with all the calories he needs for healthy growth.
        Good to remember
        Don’t ban sweets and fast food completely; let them be a special treat for your child from time to time. Most importantly, serve them only in moderate portions.
        Go out: Limit the time watching TV or sitting in front of the computer to no more than two hours a day; spend more family time doing activities such as swimming and playing fun games.
        Consult an expert: Consult a dietician to know more about healthy eating.

  • Don’t go overboard with juices
    • Children love juices because they are delicious, colourful and thirst quenching. Giving your child natural fruit juices is a better choice than giving them sweetened drinks, since juices – especially fresh juices – are rich in vitamins and minerals. They are particularly beneficial for children who don’t eat enough vegetables and fruits. However, juices should only be given in moderation.
      How much is too much?
      There is a negative side to juices, primarily because they are high in calories. This means that drinking too much juice can add unnecessary calories to your child’s diet and is why juice consumption is a major contributing factor to childhood obesity. Moreover, drinking too much juice can trigger chronic diarrhoea, gas, bloating and abdominal pains in your child because of the quick passage of carbohydrates from the juice through his delicate gastrointestinal tract. Also, the high content of sugar – even though it is natural – can lead to dental caries.
      Prioritise whole vegetables and fruits
      Juices are most often chosen as an alternative to whole vegetables and fruits. However, most juices, unless they contain lots of pieces of fruit, are much lower in fibre than the whole vegetables and fruits that they come from. Fibre is well known for helping to keep the gastrointestinal system healthy.
      Juice in moderation
      Children are the biggest consumers of juice. Some parents don’t limit how much juice their children drink because they think that juices are healthy. It’s true that 100% natural juices are a better choice than other sweetened and fruit-flavoured drinks, but parents should be careful that juices don’t replace milk, which is an essential part of your child’s diet. Children between one and two years of age consume one to two portions of fruit (one portion is equivalent to half a piece of fruit or half a cup of juice); older children should get two to four portions of fruit. For all children, juices should not make up more than half the total daily fruit portion. The best option is to offer your child a variety of fresh fruits in order to provide them with all the healthy fibre they need.
  • Keep your child safe from the negative effects of iron-deficiency anaemia
    • Anaemia caused by iron deficiency is the most common nutritional problem in the world. It is a major concern, particularly for children, because of the many negative consequences for them, including lower performance at school.
      What is the major cause of iron-deficiency anaemia?
      This form of anaemia is most often a result of not eating enough foods – such as meat – that contain iron.
      What does research say about children’s intake of meat?
      Research conducted in the Middle East shows that children in these countries do not eat enough meat to get their recommended amount iron. In fact, 85% of children in the region do not get enough meat in their diet.
      What are the symptoms of iron-deficiency anaemia?
        Pale skin: When your child’s cheeks and face appear pale
        Weakness and exhaustion: Your child will feel weak and frail and might not be able to participate in physical activity or sports at school
        Rapid heartbeat: Your child’s heartbeat will be fast, even if he is not engaging in exercise
        Headache: Your child will be prone to headaches that are not easily cured by painkillers
        Weakened immunity and repeated infections: Anaemia leads to repeated respiratory infections, like throat infections. Iron plays an important role in strengthening the body’s immune system
        Reduced school performance: Your child will lag behind his peers at school in terms of academic performance, concentration and speaking
      If you notice any of these symptoms in your child, you should consult your paediatrician as soon as possible to start a treatment that will include iron-rich foods in your child’s diet.
      How to prevent iron-deficiency anaemia in your child
      Prevention is always better than the cure, so to prevent iron-deficiency anaemia, encourage your child to eat foods rich in iron. These include:
        Meat: The most important source of iron and one that is easily absorbed by the body
        Non-meat foods: Legumes (e.g., lentils and beans) and green leafy vegetables (e.g., spinach) are rich sources of iron, but the iron isn’t absorbed by the body as well as iron from meat is. That’s why you should combine legumes and vegetables with a source of Vitamin C, which helps increase iron absorption from vegetable sources
        Fortified foods: Some foods, such as milk and breakfast cereals, often are enriched with iron. As milk plays a vital role in your child’s daily diet, iron-fortified milk helps your child meet his daily requirement of this important mineral
      In addition to all of this, you should make sure your child’s diet is balanced and varied. Try to avoid food habits that increase their risk of developing iron-deficiency anaemia, such as eating high-sugar and high-fat processed foods that provide a lot of empty calories with little nutrition.

If the immune system is weakened, for any number of reasons, your child becomes more susceptible to infection and illness.

When you start feeding your child solid foods, his diet should be balanced and contain all the nutrients needed to support a strong immune system.

You can help protect your child’s body from serious and fatal diseases by vaccinating them from birth (as per the paediatrician’s recommendation). So when your child is exposed to those germs, his body will already have the specific antibodies to kill them.

        Probiotics are live cultures that increase the level of beneficial bacteria in the digestive system and prevent the growth of harmful bacteria. As such, they are active immunity enhancers. They are found in yoghurt and fortified foods like growing-up-milk for children.

  • Bring your kids into the kitchen
    • Did you know that the earlier you teach your kids good habits, the easier it is for them to maintain these practices as they grow older? This is especially true when it comes to developing healthy eating habits as well, because the best time to start is when they are young.
      You can begin with some hands-on ways to make learning about food fun. This will not only get them interested and involved in food preparation, it will also help improve their food choices and encourage them to try new foods.
      Here are a few ways to make your kitchen a kid-friendly place!
        Start by creating an area or cupboard just for your kids. Fill the shelves with safe cooking utensils – pots, pans plastic bowls, spoons and cups, and then introduce them to cooking!
        Be prepared to be tolerant and patient (they are going to be a bit messy!) This way they will start learning about food at their own pace, while you contribute with comments about nutrition.
      Learning can be fun!
      Here’s how you can encourage them to participate in the process of cooking, while ensuring they enjoy themselves:
        Keep it clean: Let your kids wash fruits and vegetables before cooking or eating.
        Make it snappy: Children can break the stems off green beans, or snap the stalks from broccoli or cauliflower. And if they're older than 2 years, you can get them to snap open pea pods or edamame, both of which will make a very nutritious and delicious snack.
        Tear it up: Have your kids tear lettuce for salads or sandwiches.
        Slice, dice and peel: Older children can peel and slice carrots, cucumbers, potatoes. You can give them plastic knives to do so!
        Measure up: Let your kids measure flour, water, oil or milk for baking.
        A dash of fun: Show your kids how to sprinkle herbs or other seasonings onto vegetables or other mixed dishes.
        Mix smoothies: Allow them to blend milk with some of their favorite fruits, for example banana and a teaspoon of honey.
      Remember, the easier the items are to prepare, the more likely your kids will want to eat the foods again. So start out with simple pasta and vegetable dishes or let them bake muffins or home-made cakes with added fruits!
      Get them started young and you’ll see them enjoy the far-reaching benefits of eating healthy, all through their older years.
